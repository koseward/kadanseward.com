Introduction

What if one billion dollars could be pushed into the economy without any side effects whatsoever? It would be wondered why this hasn't been accomplished already. This is because it would mean that the United States would have to stop minting the penny. This isn't the first time the United States discontinued one of its coins, there is precedent. The half penny was discontinued in 1853 due to its lack of value. The same should be done for the penny because it is a significant economic drain on the mint, as well as the people who use them. Despite its popularity, the one cent coin of the United States no longer has value in our monetary system and needs to be discontinued.

Effects on the Government

When money was based on the gold or silver standard, coins had the value of its composite metals. The value of one penny was how much its weight in copper was worth, which was roughly one one-hundredth of a dollar (which was worth a predetermined amount of gold). After the United States discontinued the gold standard, the coins still needed a value. Instead of the coin being the value of its metals, it became a representative value, much like paper notes. The United States mint could now make money on the coins produced. The amount of value generated through minting is called seigniorage. As an example, a one USD note costs about 10 cents to manufacture, so the seigniorage on that dollar is 90 cents. When there is profit on the production of a coin, it makes sense for the mint to keep producing the coin. In the case of the penny, however, this hasn't been the case for more than fourteen years[3]. The first thing to note is the difference between the gross cost of the product versus the value of the one cent coins being produced. Although 80.6 million dollars was created in 2018 through the creation of pennies, it cost the mint 166.1 million. Such a transaction results in 85.5 million dollars of loss. Further, that means that each penny costs approximately 2.1 cents to make. It is also important to note that the nickel incurs negative seigniorage, at the value of 33.5 million a year.

The purchase of materials and production of coinage falls on the US government. Whose budget comes from taxes. In that respect, everyone who pays taxes in the US pays essentially a tax in order to subsidize the value of the penny, so that instead of being worth 2.1 cents, it is instead worth 1.

The Cost to the Individual

Last time I was at the grocery store, I was standing in line, waiting to check out at the self check-out machines. Despite no one visibly having very many items to scan and pay for, each time it took what I thought to be an unreasonably long time to process some of the transactions. I started to pay attention to those who lagged at the register. The fiddling with coins seemed to be one of the holdups, along with machine illiteracy. The self help registers didn't seem to be easy to use with customers, plus the customer had to find the coins in the first place. When they searched through their belongings for their pennies and dimes, it took away from time that another customer could have been checking out, and getting back to whatever they needed to be doing.

This is an important idea in economics, and a damning effect of the penny. It is called opportunity cost, and it states that something is done, something else could be done instead. So instead of the people spending time to fiddle with change, they could have instead been out of the store, getting work done or otherwise increasing the value of the world around them. Now, the average wage in the United States is about 18 dollars an hour, meaning that one second of work costs about half a cent. That the average wage of a sales clerk is about 9.60, meaning that each second they work is worth about a third of a cent. In 2001, there were an estimated 106 billion transactions every year in the United States, 36.3 percent of them involving pennies. The amount of transactions has certainly increased, but the percentage of transactions involving pennies have decreased, effectively canceling it out[6]. Taking this number, times the amount of time it costs to fiddle with pennies, which can be taken to be about two and a half seconds considering the time of the clerk, the customer and the people in line, a total of ninety-six billion seconds are wasted annually. Multiply this by the sum of the wage of the cashier and the customer, and the opportunity cost totals 733.5 million dollars per year[6]. Without the penny, all of this time is spent doing more productive endeavors.

Fallen into Disuse

The penny has such little purchasing power, and is so insignificant in regards to value, that many people in younger generations have already limited their use of the coin and coins in general. According to a survey I conducted which asked respondents how recently they have used a penny in their recent cash transactions, and whether or not they had a penny on their person. In response to the first question, more than sixty percent of respondents had neglected to use a penny in a cash transaction in more than one year, and thirty percent of people had not used a penny in more than five years. This is despite the fact that one hundred percent of people who responded to the survey have made a cash transaction in the last month.

It is one thing to not use the coins, but it is even more telling that most people did not have a penny on their person:

Despite the currency being money, and even though this is the most printed coin that the United States manufactures[3] a large majority of people do not carry them to use and facilitate commerce. Where is all of this money then? For many, their spare change, primarily pennies, is stored away in coin jars, never to be exchanged again.

So why are pennies not used in day-to-day transactions? When asked, there were many telling responses. One responder mentioned that, they're completely inconvenient. The inability to estimate a good's actual price due to the addition of sales tax at [the] point of sale makes them unusable, in my experience. Without knowing how much something costs beforehand, consumers naturally shy away from options that waste time for themselves and others. Another answerer replied, Having to dig through my bag to find pennies is really annoying, so I always end up just rounding up to the nearest dollar, giving me even MORE coins which just continues the cycle. The fact that this person has can make change, and still feels unable to is indicative of the problem. It is not a matter of not having the ability to carry all of the coins, but using them is problematic as well. So much so that it's more worth it to carry them around for no reason than to spend them.

Following by Example

The United States is not the only country to have such problems with their lowest-denomination coin. Many countries have also forsaken their one cent piece including the Eurozone, Australia, New Zealand, and even our northern neighbor, Canada.

New Zealand has gone further than many of the other countries as they have not only eliminated their one cent coin, but also their five cent piece, favoring a decimal coinage system. Their reasoning was quite elegant - it was no longer profitable. According to the New Zealand Reserve Bank, Critical to the decision (to remove our lowest denomination coins) was the cost of manufacture of the 1 cent … coin: … it cost 1.6 cents to manufacture a 1 cent coin![1] For the five cent piece, the Bank describes the coin as a nuisance and as having, no real value to the people of New Zealand due to inflation[2]. The United States is capitalist through and through, so an endeavor that is no longer profitable makes sense to stop. But perhaps New Zealand is not similar enough to the US to compare the two?

Looking at Canada, which is the most similar to the United States, in geography, history, and economic policy might offer insights to the rationale behind the decision. The economists Danu Chande and Timothy Fisher of Wilfrid Laurier University analyzed the cost of the Canadian Penny in 2003 to conclude that printing of the coin should stop, for many of the same issues outlined above for the US penny:

First, the negative seigniorage that the penny caused for the Royal Canadian Mint is noted. Each penny, in 2000 and 2001 respectively, cost 4.95 and 3.95 cents to manufacture. This led to an overall loss of over 50 million dollars between the two years. The opportunity cost, or user cost, as referred to in the journal, was also calculated. Chande and Fisher estimate that the time cost for using pennies is approximately 1.5 seconds as opposed to transactions without pennies. Assuming that in Canada there are more than 40 million transactions per day, there would be an opportunity cost of 129 million dollars a year[1].

This data, becoming more and more significant as years went by is why Canada stopped producing its one cent coin in 2012. These countries should be models to the United States moving forwards.

A Solution

Before, it was mentioned that the nickel resulted in negative seigniorage at the US Mint in addition to the penny. Assuming that removing the five cent piece is not an option, as the economic costs are not as well known as that of the penny, there exists a solution that solves the problem of the penny and that of the negative seigniorage. Instead of ceasing the production of the penny, which has a relatively low cost of production compared to the other coins when not compared to face value, the minting of nickels could halt. Then, pennies would have the value of nickels, and nickels already in circulation would still be legal tender, while banks collect them over time, selling them to the US Mint for melting. The effect of this would be that the five cent coin, at least in the short term, no longer produces negative seigniorage and that there is no one cent coin wasting people's time. There is the cost of heightened inflation with such a solution, but the cost is easily overtaken by the savings generated annually from such an act[4].

Lombra's Counterargument

Whenever the abolition of the penny is brought up, Raymond Lombra's argument is the most credible research based paper of the opposition, so it is prudent to analyze this argument. Lombra, of Penn. State University argued in a famous paper from 2001 that eliminating the penny from the US coinage system would have an immediate effect on consumers and disproportionately affect poorer areas of the country.

Lombra begins with a data collection of nearly 4000 items and their prices from a chain convenience store, finding that nearly 82 percent of them have a price ending in 9. He then simulates 5000 transactions between one and three items from the above list and implements rounding to predict how rounding would affect consumers. Lombra found that between 60 and 90 percent of these transactions rounded up, resulting in a loss for consumers[5].

The next issue Lombra brings up is that the elimination of the penny would increase prices. Due to the above rounding tax when taken against the total spending of American consumers, the rounding would increase prices by .01 of a percent per year. Lombra argues that while on individual prices, the difference is un-noticeable, the sum of the effect raises prices significantly[5].

Rebuttal

The primary issue with Lombra's argument is the date of publication. 2001 was a long time ago and there has been a lot of research since on the topic. It was determined that the simulations that Lombra ran were not representative of the purchases of real consumers. Additionally, Lombra failed to include sales tax in his calculations. Only 2.5 percent of Americans shop without sales tax, not representative of the purchasing base. Whaples argued in 2007 that with the inclusion of sales tax completely erases the distribution that Lombra found. Whaples analyzed 185 thousand transactions across twenty stores across America, and found the opposite findings of Lombra. Instead of the consumer losing out on these transactions, it was found that the customers earned 105.6 dollars. Of course, this is not any major money exchange, only about one-twentieth of a cent per transaction. The loss to the store is not significant either, only about 75 cents per day. In the state with the worst case, Pennsylvania which is one state where the consumer does lose out on rounding - an astounding one cent for every 67 transactions. These stores come from a variety of socio-economic areas, and so these results are not affected by social status[6].

To address Lombra's concern regarding the increase in prices, the value discussed cannot be accurate. The increase in prices was assumed due to the rounding tax that was believed to have existed. However, because this tax could not be supported, it stands to reason that this inflation would not occur. Even if this inflation did happen for some unseen variable one time, at a level that would not affect the economy. The Consumer Price Index, which is reported by the Bureau of Labor Statistics only reports data to the first decimal point, so the 1/100 of a percent increase would not be reported in most cases. This indicates that the change is so insignificant as to not matter[6].

Conclusion

Due to the seigniorage of the coin and the effect on consumers, it is time to rid the United States of its lowest denomination coin. It makes sense now, and has made sense for many years now, and the fact that it has not is embarrassing. Strong capitalist policy is the signature of the United States and balancing the budget is important to everyone. If the country was serious about it, perhaps it would consider such wasteful spending as no longer necessary. Almost nowhere else does Congress consistently vote to throw away money in such a direct manner. Minting the penny is simply wasting money - to have the opportunity to lose more money.

Any other solution to combat these effects is simply a short term band-aid that does nothing in the long term. This is a problem that is easily dealt with now and so should be done so. There is no reason to hold off on such a simple solution.
