Microservices

Overview

Microservices are a popular Software Architecture, often used in modern, large-scale web services, such as Amazon or Netflix.[5] As described by Red Hat, a major open source developer, Microservices involve breaking down a larger software endeavor into smaller pieces, which are then developed individually in smaller developmental teams and are individually releasable.[2] Each of these services are fine-grained, and have a low level of coupling.[6] Often these sections are separate, and are not accessible to teams, giving each piece of the system a privacy that other architectures lack.

A depiction of a Microservice architecture

History

Microservices are a type of the Service Oriented Architecture. The difference being that while SOA involve all of the systems of a company, microservices involve only the pieces of an individual system.[6]

Characteristics

  • Modularity: Microservices are stored in several separate repositories.[6]
  • Privacy: Due to the modularity of the architecture, each service can be developed in small teams. Thus there can be parts of the system that some developers do not have access to.[1]
  • Independent Release: Because of the modularity of microservices, and low coupling, each package can be released on its own, without the rest of the system. This means that code reuse is much easier.[2]

Comparison with other Architectures

  • Vs. Monolithic Architectures:

    Complexity is a major difference between the Monolith and Microservices. While the Monolith exists in a single repository, Microservices have a repository for each of the services. The increase in complexity comes with greater cognitive load for managers. There is also increased deployment complexity. The release of the software is much more complicated and requires automation, while the monolith has a comparatively simple deployment.[3]

    Performance is also to be considered when choosing an architecture. In many ways, the monolith is superior. When handling requests, the monolith, at all amounts, was faster than that of the microservices. This is often attributed to the gateways of the microservices wasting resources, while the monolith does not have these wastes. Even when there are services handling the microservices, the performance is still lacking.[4]

    Cost is cheaper when a microservices is implemented. Infrastructure costs, as well as the cost of technical debt is significantly lower when implementing microservices. Infrastructure costs refer to the cost of running a server, and microservices have costs up to 60% less than the monolith.[5] And whereas technical debt increases exponentially in monoliths, they increase linearly with microservices. Thus, the cost is much lower.[7]
